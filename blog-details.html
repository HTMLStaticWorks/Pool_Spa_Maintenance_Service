Understanding Swimming Pool pH Dynamics

Dr. Charles Miller Lead Water Biochemist, AquaElite

Maintaining a swimming pool or luxury spa involves more than just skimming leaves and vacuuming sediment. The true foundation of crystal-clear water lies in its microscopic biochemistry. Among all chemical measurements, the potential of Hydrogen (pH) is arguably the most critical parameter to monitor. The pH scale measures how acidic or basic your pool water is, operating on a logarithmic scale from 0 to 14, with 7.0 representing absolute neutrality.

The Perfect Balance Window (7.2 to 7.6)

For swimming pool water, the ideal range is strictly bounded between 7.2 and 7.6. This specific window is not selected arbitrarily; it aligns directly with the natural pH of human eyes and skin (approximately 7.4). Swimmers experience discomfort and red eyes when water drifts outside this range, regardless of chlorine volumes.

"When pH levels surge above 7.8, the sanitizing efficacy of standard free chlorine drops by over 50%. You are essentially throwing away half of your sanitizer budget."

Consequences of Unbalanced pH

If water pH drops below 7.2, the water becomes acidic. Acidic water is highly corrosive, gradually eating away at the copper heat exchangers in pool heaters, etching concrete plaster walls, and wrinkling vinyl liners. Conversely, if pH levels climb above 7.6, the water becomes alkaline and scale-forming. This triggers calcium precipitation, turning the water cloudy, clogging filters, and leaving hard white calcium lines along tiles.

Pro-Active pH Control Checklist

  • Check Alkalinity First: Total Alkalinity acts as a buffer. Keep it between 80–120 ppm before adjusting pH.
  • Use Sodium Bisulfate to Lower: Add pH Decreaser slowly in front of return lines with pumps running.
  • Use Soda Ash to Raise: Only add pH Increaser when levels drop below 7.2.
  • Log Digitally: Keep active records of weekly checks to observe mechanical scaling trends.
